üçú North East Cuisine: A Culinary Map

Discover the unique, bold, and flavourful gastronomy of the seven sister states and Sikkim. Prepare your palate for a true feast!

Assam (Axomiya)

Characterized by light preparation with little spice and strong aromatic herbs. Must-try: **Khar** (a dish with a signature alkaline ingredient), **Tenga** (sour fish curry), and **Pithas** (rice cakes).

Nagaland (Naga)

Famous for its liberal use of smoked meat and fermented products. Try: **Smoked Pork with Axone** (fermented soybean) or **Bamboo Shoot** (Baste-tenga), often accompanied by fiery King Chilli.

Manipur (Meitei)

Known for its clean, oil-less cooking style and abundance of fish and vegetables. Key dishes include **Eromba** (a vegetable-fish mash) and **Singju** (a spicy fermented salad).

Meghalaya (Khasi, Garo)

Staple food is rice, often accompanied by meat, especially pork. Delicacies: **Jadoh** (rice cooked with meat), **Doh-Khleh** (pork salad), and **Nakham Bitchi** (Garo soup).

Mizoram (Mizo)

The cuisine is generally mild but can be made spicy with chilies. Must-have: **Bai** (a vegetable stew), **Misa Mach Poora** (grilled shrimp), and **Vawksa Rep** (smoked pork).

Arunachal Pradesh

Simple preparations with minimal spices, relying on fermentation and smoking. Key dishes: **Apong** (rice beer), **Lukter** (meat cooked with King Chilli), and various **Thukpas** and Momos.

Tripura (Tripuri)

Traditional cuisine often uses **Berma** (fermented fish) as a key flavoring agent. Try: **Mui Borok** (the classic Tripuri meal) and **Chakhwi** (bamboo shoot stew).

Sikkim (Lepcha, Bhutia)

Popular for its Himalayan cuisine influences. Signature dishes: **Momos** (dumplings), **Thukpa** (noodle soup), **Sha Phaley** (bread stuffed with meat/cheese), and locally brewed beverages.

**Did you know?** Many North Eastern kitchens use far less oil compared to other Indian regions, favoring boiling, smoking, and fermenting to preserve and enhance the natural flavors of the ingredients.

🥡 Traditional Attire & Handloom

The region is a textile powerhouse. Explore the vibrant colours, intricate weaves, and unique dressing styles that define each state's cultural identity.

Assam

Famous for three types of indigenous silk: **Muga**, **Eri**, and **Pat**. Women wear the **Mekhela Chadar**, a two-piece drape, while men wear the **Suria** (dhoti) and **Chadar** (shawl).

Nagaland

Known for its distinctive, heavy, and colourful **Shawls** that signify status and tribe, such as the **Tsungkotepsu** (Ao tribe) and **Rongkhim** (Yimchunger tribe). The attire is often paired with elaborate beadwork and metal accessories.

Manipur

The women wear the beautiful **Phanek** (a handloom wrap-around skirt) and the **Innaphi** (a delicate shawl). The Lai-Phi, worn by deities, is a spectacular sight.

Meghalaya

Khasi women wear the **Jainsem** and **Jainkup** (a dress-like cloth). Garo women wear a wrap-around called **Dhak-Munda**, characterized by bold stripes and colourful geometric patterns.

Mizoram

The iconic **Puanchei** is a colourful, intricately woven skirt, worn during major festivals. The **Puan** is the common wrap-around textile, with designs often reflecting their ancestry.

Arunachal Pradesh

Varies significantly by tribe. The Monpas wear woven skull-caps with tassels, and their women wear long, sleeveless coats over full-sleeved blouses, often accessorized with silver ornaments.

**Handloom Heritage:** The textiles of the North East are primarily hand-woven, reflecting a deep, unbroken tradition of craftsmanship. Each pattern and colour often carries a specific symbolic meaning related to the tribe, clan, or occasion.
